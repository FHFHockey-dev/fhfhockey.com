# Sustainability in NHL Player Performance

## The right way to define sustainability

For NHL analysis, **sustainability** is best understood as the probability that a playerŌĆÖs current production rate will continue once you strip out luck, score effects, teammate/opponent context, and other short-sample noise. In practice, the most sustainable indicators are not recent goals or points by themselves but the **repeatable processes that create them**: shot volume, shot quality, playmaking volume, deployment, and role. Academic and public-hockey work converge on the same basic point: because goals are sparse and noisy, shot-based and expected-goal-based measures are usually more useful than raw results for forecasting what comes next. ## Building a player baseline

For both short-term and season-long forecasting, the best baseline is not a simple season-to-date average, not a raw career average, and not a pure recency average. The most reliable approach is a **weighted multi-year prior** that gives more weight to recent seasons, then **regresses back toward a positional mean**, and then **applies an age adjustment**. That is the basic Marcel-style philosophy behind hockey forecasting, and it is also consistent with modern projection systems that train on multiple prior seasons and find that the strongest predictors of a future metric are usually the playerŌĆÖs own recent versions of that same metric. For goaltenders, this kind of shrinkage is not optional. In public Marcel-style goalie work, recent seasons are weighted more than older ones, then the estimate is pushed hard back toward league average because raw goalie performance is so volatile. In the updated Hockey Marcels work, goalies in the sample were regressed by about 40% to the mean on average, with smaller-sample goalies regressed much more heavily. Age also needs to be individualized rather than treated as a one-size-fits-all decline curve. Recent work using functional principal component analysis stresses that age is a major feature in hockey forecasting and that players have **individual aging curves**, not one universal path. Earlier literature cited in that work places forward peak age around 27 to 28 and defensemen around 28 to 29, while WAR-based public skater curves often show a broader early-to-mid-20s plateau and then gradual decline. ## Skater signals that usually persist

For **forwards**, the strongest sustainability signals are usually: individual shot volume, individual expected goals, power-play role, and playmaking volume that shows up before assists do. The Annual Review survey of hockey analytics reports that individual expected goals outperform individual Corsi and individual goals in predictive ability for individual scoring, and that with 20 or more games observed, xG values become more predictive of future goals than Corsi or goals alone. Evolving-HockeyŌĆÖs standard skater tables separate these pieces cleanly into individual shots, individual expected goals, and on-ice xG share, which is exactly the foundation a forecasting model needs. For **defensemen**, the signal mix is different. Defensemen usually drive fantasy offense less through pure finishing and more through **transition and facilitation**. Public passing-data work has shown that defensemen who are on the ice for entries that directly lead to shots have repeatable, predictive value for future scoring, and that entry-assist-style transition metrics are especially informative for evaluating how well a defenseman moves play from defense to offense. MoneyPuckŌĆÖs ŌĆ£created expected goals,ŌĆØ which gives added credit for shots likely to create rebound opportunities, is also especially helpful here because it tends to give more credit to defensemen than plain xG does. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn23view2Ņłéturn20view4Ņłéturn31view3Ņłü

For **playmaking**, shot-assist-derived metrics are far more useful than ordinary assist totals. Hockey Graphs work on expected primary points found that shot-assist rates carry more predictive information than a playerŌĆÖs own shot totals in some settings, and that expected primary-assist concepts substantially outperform raw primary assists as measures of playmaking. A second Hockey Graphs study found that **shots plus shot assists**, taken together as shot contributions, are better predictors of future performance than shots alone. That is one of the clearest answers to your question about what most indicates sustainability for skaters: if a point producer is also piling up shot contributions, the production is much more believable. The main **unsustainability flags** for skaters are the opposite. The Annual Review article notes that, for the vast majority of NHL players, shooting percentage regresses toward the positional mean, and that **secondary assists** are much noisier than primary assists and goals because they are more influenced by randomness and team effects. Team- or line-level PDO is useful mainly as a regression flag: very high shooting percentage plus save percentage tends to pull back toward average over time even if a team or player can remain somewhat above average by talent. For defensemen specifically, public work has repeatedly warned that on-ice save percentage is not stable enough to be used as a reliable player-evaluation input. ## Streaks, regression, and breakout detection

A player is most likely to **return to normal** when the gap between results and process is wide but the process has not changed. The cleanest examples are a scorer with strong ixG and shot volume but a depressed shooting percentage, or a playmaker with strong shot-assist and expected-primary-point rates but few assists. That same logic works in reverse: a player riding an elevated goal pace without a matching rise in ixG, shots, or role is the classic candidate to cool off. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn34view0Ņłéturn23view0Ņłéturn23view1Ņłü

A genuine **breakout** is most believable when there is both a process change and a role change. In practical fantasy terms, the strongest breakout signals are a sustained jump in ATOI, first-unit power-play access, an increase in shot rate or ixG/60, and a better teammate environment. For **defensemen**, breakout detection should be even more role-sensitive. A rising points pace from a defenseman is far more believable if it is accompanied by an increase in power-play quarterbacking, shot-assist volume, or transition involvement. Public defenseman work on Hockey Graphs argues that entry assists leading directly to shots are both repeatable and predictive, which makes them a much better ŌĆ£this is realŌĆØ indicator than a defenseman simply shooting 11% from the blue line for a month. A **cold streak coming** is usually visible before the points fully disappear. The biggest tells are declining shot volume, flattening or falling ixG/60, loss of first-unit power-play time, tougher quality of competition, or a drop in teammate quality. RAPM is useful here because it was designed to control for teammate, opponent, zone-start, and score-state effects; if the raw box score is slipping but the playerŌĆÖs adjusted impact looks stable and the role is unchanged, the ŌĆ£cold streakŌĆØ may just be noise. A **hot streak ending** usually looks like some combination of: goals well above ixG, shooting percentage far above a regressed player baseline, a run of secondary assists, or line-level PDO that is carrying the production. A **cold streak ending** looks like the inverse: strong shot and xG creation with bad conversion, strong playmaking process with weak teammate finishing, or restored role after a temporary demotion. One especially useful short-horizon insight comes from passing-data work showing that in small samples, **primary shot assists can predict future primary assists better than primary assists themselves**, with the edge peaking around an eight-game sample before raw assists eventually catch up. ## Goalie-specific forecasting

Goalie sustainability has to start with one uncomfortable truth: **raw save percentage is noisy**, and wins and goals-against average are heavily contaminated by team context. Evolving-HockeyŌĆÖs projection write-up is unusually blunt on this point, calling save percentage one of the most unreliable and unrepeatable metrics they had to model and arguing that it is often better to model the underlying raw statistics instead of the ratio itself. The broader hockey-analytics literature says much the same thing: to evaluate goalies meaningfully, you need to compare their results with the shot quality they faced. The core public goalie metrics are therefore **expected goals against**, **expected save percentage**, **goals saved above expected**, and **save percentage above expected**. ## Outside factors, short-term fantasy edges, and prediction markets

The biggest outside factors for skaters after raw talent are **deployment, teammate quality, opponent quality, schedule, and health**. Evolving-HockeyŌĆÖs QoT/QoC framework formalizes teammate and opponent strength as weighted averages based on ice-time overlap, while RAPM exists precisely because raw on-ice numbers are distorted by teammates, opponents, zone starts, back-to-backs, and score state. Schedule effects are real enough to matter at fantasy horizons. For a **one- to two-week fantasy edge**, the public evidence suggests a simple hierarchy. First, update role: who is on PP1, who is getting the extra minute and a half, who is now playing with better finishers, who is likely to start in goal. Second, use process stats from the recent window: shots, ixG, shot assists, entry assists for defensemen, and quality-adjusted goalie metrics. Third, regress outcomes back toward baseline: shooting percentage, save percentage, secondary assists, and PDO-style spikes are the first things to fade. Fourth, layer in schedule and fatigue. For **season-long forecasting**, the most reliable public recipe is to model the raw fantasy categories directly. Prediction markets and sportsbooks can be useful, but mostly as **calibration devices**, not as replacements for your model. Research on NHL moneyline odds movement found little statistically significant difference in predictive power across the life of the market, though odds on game day tended to be a bit more accurate in the aggregate. The same thesis also found evidence of favorite-longshot bias. Meanwhile, historical modeling work has shown that structured Poisson-style hockey models can sometimes outperform the market in backtests. The sober takeaway is that the market is an informative consensus prior, especially close to game time, but not an oracle; if you build a player-prop or fantasy model, market prices are best treated as a strong external input that can tell you when your number is probably off. The broad gap in public NHL forecasting is that puck- and player-tracking data still have not been fully folded into everyday fantasy models. Research on tracking-based hockey analytics has already proposed passing-lane, passing-effectiveness, and pressure metrics that were not previously possible in hockey and that should support more accurate future performance models.
