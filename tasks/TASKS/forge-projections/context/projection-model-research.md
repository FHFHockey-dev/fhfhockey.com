# FORGE research synthesis for NHL projection modeling

## What matters most for your project

The strongest cross-source signal is that **FORGE should treat prediction as a matchup-and-state problem, not just a descriptive stats problem**. The best resources repeatedly push toward one canonical row per game or matchup, opponent-relative or home-minus-away feature design, rolling and recency-weighted inputs, and explicit handling of home ice, rest, special teams, and starting-goalie context. They also consistently warn that raw same-game stats and duplicated team-perspective rows can create leakage, contradictory outputs, or unrealistically good backtests. citeturn1view0turn9view2turn10view0turn11view1turn12view1turn15view0

The second big theme is that **data freshness is an operational problem as much as a modeling problem**. Within the resources you shared, lineup news, starting goalies, and injuries are frequently described as scraped, manually curated, or inferred from beat-writer/news sources rather than cleanly provided by an official league feed. That matters directly for your `update-goalie-projections-v2`, `update-line-combinations`, `start-chart`, and `goalies.ts` paths: if the starter signal is weak, the rest of the model stack can be mathematically sound and still miss the slate. citeturn19view0turn19view1turn19view2turn20view0turn20view1turn21view0

The third theme is that **goalie prediction needs a separate uncertainty philosophy from skaters and teams**. MoneyPuck’s current public design explicitly separates a team’s “ability to win,” “scoring chances,” and “goaltending,” and its starting-goalie model leans on usage and deployment variables such as days rest, recent workload, age, recent full-game share, and multi-year GSAx/Sv% rather than assuming that “elite goalie talent” is stable by itself. That approach fits the much older and broader volatility literature in your list, which repeatedly finds weak season-to-season repeatability for goalie outcomes. citeturn11view2turn27view0turn27view2turn17view3turn25view0turn25view1

The last major theme is that **consistency is useful, but mostly as a secondary layer**. The team-level consistency work suggests average team strength matters more than game-to-game variance; the forward-level consistency work suggests some metrics have enough “stickiness” to support consistency badges or stabilization priors; and the goalie literature suggests consistency itself is fragile enough that it should feed uncertainty bands more than hard point estimates. In practical FORGE terms, consistency belongs in `uncertainty.ts`, `goalieModel.ts`, and the UI’s confidence language more than in a naive “hot/cold” multiplier. citeturn18view0turn18view1turn2view0turn27view3

## Resource digest

### Forecasting and feature engineering

**Resource 1. SportsAnalytics Reddit thread.** This is a very good schema warning. The most useful advice is to stop framing a game-level target with two opposing team-perspective rows, because the model can produce contradictory outputs on the same game and effectively learn each instance twice; the stronger suggestions are to store one row per game and, for scoring-event targets, consider decomposing the outcome or using Poisson-style modeling. citeturn1view0

**Resource 2. Buttrey’s JSM paper.** This is one of the cleanest “classical” game-outcome frameworks in your list. It models scoring and yielding with independent Poisson processes, models penalties separately, feeds those rates into Markov/simulation logic, and reports a positive betting ROI, including 2.6% in the 2014-15 test season; it also explicitly ignores empty-net goals and treats 6-on-5 goals as ordinary 5-on-5 for practicality, which is a reminder that special game states can easily contaminate broader-strength estimates if they are not bucketed carefully. citeturn3view1turn23view1turn0view0

**Resource 3. Hockey-Statistics variable-testing article.** This is highly relevant to `buildStrengthTablesV2` and `run-forge-projections.ts` because it tests variables by actual predictive impact using log loss instead of just descriptive appeal. The article builds game-basis player data split by EV/PP/SH, creates “impact” measures relative to role/position averages, then finds that offensive forward indicators such as individual goals, GF, and xGF are among the best single predictors, while penalty-drawn/taken metrics add little predictive value. citeturn5view1

**Resource 4. Hockey-Graphs predictive-modelling tag archive.** This is not one method paper so much as a curated library of modeling directions. The archive surfaces work on recurrent neural networks for prospect projection, adjusted possession, context-sensitive Corsi, faceoff value, and related predictive modeling work; for FORGE, its value is less in one deployable algorithm and more in showing the breadth of usable hockey features beyond box-score goals and assists. citeturn4view1

**Resource 5. 8rain Station walkthrough.** This is more product methodology than statistical methodology, but it is still useful. The strongest ideas are to treat the model as a baseline probability engine rather than a command, to track Brier score, log loss, and calibration explicitly, and to combine model output with independent market or stats-weight signals when choosing where to act; that logic maps well to your existing `run-projection-accuracy` and `goalies.ts` metadata/calibration surfaces. citeturn6view1

**Resource 6. Markov-chains hockey paper.** This article is much more relevant to live win probability than to pregame player projection, but it is still strategically useful. It builds continuous-time Markov state models using shot differential, goal differential, and manpower states, then finds that goal differential is by far the dominant in-game driver, shot differential adds information, and splitting manpower into many separate states adds less than you might expect because those states are sparse; it also recommends future integration of shot quality, PBP events, and overtime handling. citeturn7view0turn8view0turn23view3

**Resource 7. Data Science Stack Exchange thread.** This is the best player-count modeling resource in the list. The useful contribution is the hierarchical Bayesian framing: goals can be treated as Poisson counts with games-played offsets, player-level partial pooling, and extensions for time, position, team, and shift context—exactly the kind of shrinkage logic that reduces overreaction to small samples in player projections. citeturn9view2turn9view4

**Resource 8. Darryl Blackport’s NHL prediction model.** This is one of the most directly actionable game-model writeups in the list. It uses season-holdout cross-validation to avoid leakage across seasons, reduces 5,000-plus variables down to about 100 through feature selection and out-of-sample validation, then ensembles logistic regression, CatBoost, and naive Bayes; its final feature set mixes rolling xG, possession, WAR/Game Score, rest effects, and starting-goalie variables, and it openly notes that early-season predictions were weaker. citeturn10view0

**Resource 9. Christian Lee’s Random Forest article.** The useful part here is not the reported ~70% accuracy by itself; it is the author’s own recognition that some strong features are likely informative because they contain current-game goal information. The article flags that metrics like same-game 5v5 save percentage can inflate accuracy, and its stated next step is to remove goal-containing features and rely only on prior information—exactly the right leakage discipline for FORGE. citeturn22view3turn22view4

**Resource 15. Gary Schwaeber’s model-building article.** This piece is valuable because it explains the data architecture of a top-down team model: team logs for 5v5, PP, PK; goalie game logs; rolling point-in-time statistics; and an ELO feature merged against official schedule/results. It is especially relevant to your `ingest-projection-inputs`, `buildStrengthTablesV2`, and `update-goalie-projections-v2` stages because it shows how many “simple” game models really depend on careful rolling joins and point-in-time feature creation. citeturn11view1

**Resource 16. MoneyPuck about page.** This is probably the single most directly reusable public blueprint in the list. MoneyPuck’s current pregame model is explicitly decomposed into team “ability to win,” “scoring chances,” and “goaltending,” with goaltending weighted at 29%, scoring chances at 54%, and ability to win at 17%; its separate starting-goalie model uses days rest, last-game result, recent full-game share, multi-year workload, age, two-year GSAx/Sv%, home/away, roster depth, and game importance. citeturn11view2

**Resource 17. JNoel71 GitHub model.** This repo is a very practical engineering reference. It uses over 600 features derived from multiple rolling windows, splits the season into early/middle/late models, represents features as home-minus-away differences, and reports log loss of 0.6660 in cross-validation and 0.6526 on the 2021 holdout season using ExtraTrees; that kind of staging and multi-window design is a strong template for FORGE’s derived tables and matchup assembly. citeturn12view1turn12view2

**Resource 18. DRatings.** The public methodology is deliberately simple but still informative: ratings are based around logistic regression and Bradley-Terry logic, then converted to matchup probabilities with sport-specific adjustments. Its public pages also expose probabilities, projected totals, and log-loss tracking, which makes it useful less as a modeling frontier and more as a reminder that users trust products that publish both predictions and scoring of those predictions. citeturn15view0turn13view0turn13view1

### Data sourcing and product operations

**Resource 10. Reddit thread on lineup APIs.** This is operationally important and lines up with real-world pain. The thread’s useful consensus is that there is no clean official NHL feed for lineup news in the way engineers want, which is why projects resort to beat-writer tweets, manually maintained sites, or scraping Daily Faceoff/PuckPedia-style pages; for FORGE, this means starter/lineup inputs should have **source provenance and confidence levels**, not just values. citeturn19view0turn19view1turn19view2

**Resource 11. Reddit thread on NHL injury status.** This one is accessible, but substantively weak. Its main value is negative: it shows that even developers looking directly at NHL API fields like `rosterStatus` found injury semantics unclear, which reinforces that injury ingestion should not rely on one opaque field without validation or an alternate source. citeturn19view3turn24view2

**Resource 12. Django betting-site thread.** The most useful detail here is operational honesty: the site’s author says starting goalies were the one thing still manually selected each day, while team and goalie stats were automated via multiple NHL API endpoints, Hockey Reference injury scraping, and Django cron jobs. That is a strong warning that “starter truth” often remains a human-in-the-loop problem even in otherwise automated stacks. citeturn20view0turn20view1

**Resource 13. Webdev NHL stats site thread.** This is mainly product inspiration, but still helpful. The author spent two years building an NHL stats UI, relied mostly on the official NHL API plus an injury endpoint from 9c9media, and got strong positive feedback on the interface—along with critiques on table responsiveness; for FORGE, the lesson is that users care about data freshness, accessibility, and clean presentation almost as much as model quality. citeturn21view0

**Resource 14. Wildhockey injury-chart thread.** This is not a modeling source, but it is a strong UI idea. The user is explicitly asking for a player-on-y-axis and game-on-x-axis missed-games visualization—the hockey equivalent of an injury Gantt chart—which would fit very naturally into a FORGE context as a “why did the line combinations or team strength change?” explainer view. citeturn20view4turn20view5

### Consistency and goalie uncertainty

**Resource 19. Hockey-Graphs team consistency article.** This is the cleanest pushback against casual “consistency” narratives. Using team Corsi-per-20 and standard deviation, it finds that the spread in average performance across teams is much larger than the spread in consistency, implying that teams mostly differ in how good they are on average, not in some dramatic hidden consistency trait. citeturn18view0turn18view3

**Resource 20. CMU forwards consistency report.** This is a genuinely useful framework for player-level consistency scoring. The authors examine metric “stickiness” across seasons, keep even-strength goals, even-strength assists, and shooting percentage because they were among the more predictable measures, convert metrics to /60, use z-scores plus PCA-derived weights, and define consistency through weighted mean change across seasons; they also find salary is related to performance more than consistency. citeturn2view0

**Resource 21. Matt Desfosses on goalie predictability.** This is more descriptive than causal, but it is still valuable. Using era-adjusted save percentage back to 1979-80 and minimum shot thresholds to suppress noise, it shows that consistency can be summarized by save-percentage standard deviation, and that truly consistent goalies are rare enough that a “consistency badge” is more defensible than a strong year-ahead point forecast. citeturn25view0turn25view1turn25view3

**Resource 22. Reddit thread on goalie volatility.** This is anecdotal, but directionally consistent with the better evidence. The discussion centers on the idea that goaltending is unusually volatile and that expensive long-term goalie bets are dangerous, with commenters explicitly arguing that “consistently elite” goaltending is rare or non-existent in the modern NHL. citeturn19view4turn19view5

**Resource 23. Bryant contract-year thesis.** This is a surprisingly useful “don’t assume motivation hacks work” source. In a dataset of 2,717 player-season observations with controls for age, position, team strength, games played, and year effects, the thesis finds that players tended to underperform across several offensive and defensive categories during contract years, which is the opposite of the usual “bet on the contract-year bump” story. citeturn26view0turn26view1

**Resource 24. JFresh on goaltending randomness.** This is one of the most important resources in the entire list for your goalie model. The piece argues that season-to-season goalie performance is barely repeatable, reports correlations around 0.12 for season-to-season save-percentage-above-expected and save-percentage-based rates, and also shows weak within-season playoff carryover; the practical implication is that a goalie model should lean hard on uncertainty, regression, and role probability rather than on a single confident point estimate. citeturn27view0turn27view1turn27view2turn27view3

**Resource 25. NHL Moneyball Goalie Analysis paper.** This is more roster-value research than daily prediction research, but it still contributes a useful variable shortlist. The paper uses 2018-19 through 2022-23 data, notes prior literature that year-to-year goalie consistency is highly random, then finds win-percentage signal in variables such as age, games played, GAA, save percentage, GA/60, xGA/60, GSAx/60, and Corsi%; those are good candidates for longer-horizon goalie talent priors even if they are not enough by themselves for next-game certainty. citeturn3view6turn3view7turn23view5

**Resource 26. Expected Buffalo’s goalie trend search.** This is a useful bridge between the “goalies are random” camp and a more nuanced operational view. The author finds many layers of randomness, sees no clear age trend, treats high-danger save percentage as a peripheral rather than core evaluation metric, warns hard against paying for outlier seasons, and still concludes that the best long-run goalies are usually the more consistent ones; that combination points toward **volatility-aware priors**, not abandonment of analysis. citeturn17view1turn17view3turn17view4

## What the resources imply for FORGE

Your current FORGE architecture already has the right factory-shaped spine: ingest, derive, project, grade, serve, and render. The biggest upgrade suggested by the resource set is not a total rewrite; it is a **tighter separation between descriptive inputs, predictive features, and uncertainty layers**. For the ingest/derived side, the literature strongly favors recording state-aware signals cleanly—EV/PP/PK splits, shot-quality context, home/rest context, goalie context, and point-in-time rolling windows—while keeping edge cases like empty-net and 6-on-5 events out of the same buckets that define core team or player strength. That argues for enriching `pbp.ts`, `shifts.ts`, `buildStrengthTablesV2.ts`, and `buildGoalieGameV2.ts` with explicit flags and wider derived-window coverage rather than trying to “fix” everything later in the projection runner. citeturn3view1turn0view0turn5view1turn11view1turn12view1

For `run-forge-projections.ts`, the best synthesis is a **hybrid structure**: a team-strength layer, a scoring-chance layer, and a goalie layer, each estimated from rolling historical windows and merged at matchup time. That is very close to the public MoneyPuck framing and also consistent with Bradley-Terry/logistic game models, feature-differential tree models, and Poisson-based score generation. In practical code terms, that means you should favor home-minus-away matchup features, multiple recency windows, and stage-of-season priors over giant undifferentiated feature dumps; it also means the projection runner should know whether a feature was built from prior information only, because several resources show how easily current-game information can leak into apparently strong models. citeturn11view2turn12view1turn10view0turn22view3turn15view0

For `goalieModel.ts` and `update-goalie-projections-v2.ts`, the resource set points very clearly toward a **two-step or mixture design**. Step one is starter probability, driven by rest, usage, recent starts, age, team context, and roster depth. Step two is performance conditional on starter identity, but with aggressive shrinkage and much wider uncertainty than you would use for skaters. If FORGE currently produces a single clean ranked goalie list, it should probably also produce a hidden or surfaced mixture distribution that says, in effect, “starter identity is X% one goalie, Y% another, and each branch has different save/win/shutout distributions.” That would align much better with the volatility evidence in your source set. citeturn11view2turn27view0turn27view3turn17view3turn25view0

For `reconcile.ts` and `uncertainty.ts`, the open opportunity is to use consistency and volatility research as **distribution shapers**. Team-level consistency work says average strength matters more than volatility, so team priors should be fairly stable. Skater-level consistency work says some measures are stickier than others, so player uncertainty bands should widen or narrow depending on metric type, recency, and sample size. Goalie work says the tails should be much fatter. In other words, uncertainty should not be a uniform simulation wrapper; it should know the difference between stable team shot-generation, moderately sticky forward creation metrics, and chaotic goalie outcomes. citeturn18view1turn2view0turn27view2turn26view0

For `run-projection-accuracy.ts`, `accuracy.ts`, and the metadata returned by `goalies.ts`, the resources strongly support **grading probabilities, not just picks**. Several of the sources work in log loss, Brier score, cross-validation splits, and calibration thinking. Your existing mention of calibration snapshots is exactly the right direction. The next step is to make those diagnostics more granular: by position, by stat type, by confidence bucket, by starter-certainty bucket, by early/mid/late season, and by whether the eventual starter matched the model prior. citeturn6view1turn10view0turn12view2turn13view1turn22view3

For the UI layer in `FORGE.tsx` and `start-chart.ts`, the sources suggest a product advantage beyond raw predictions: **explain why the number moved**. The webdev and Reddit UI threads point toward responsive cards, better table handling, and visual timeline components. The strongest new FORGE views would likely be a starter-confidence badge, a lineup-freshness badge with source provenance, an injury timeline or man-games-lost strip, and “trend” components that distinguish performance trend from statistical confidence. Those features would make the existing projection cards and uncertainty ranges much more actionable. citeturn21view0turn20view4turn20view5turn19view2

## Where the biggest gaps are

- **A canonical matchup schema is still the first gap to close.** If any part of FORGE’s training data is effectively storing the same matchup twice from opposite perspectives for a game-level target, you are inviting contradictory outputs, target leakage, and unstable feature importance. The safest default is one row per game or market, with clearly separated home and away features or explicit differences. citeturn1view0turn12view1

- **You likely need a formal feature-leakage registry.** Several resources either directly warn about same-game stat leakage or quietly demonstrate it through abnormally high measured performance. Every feature in `buildStrengthTablesV2.ts` and downstream assemblies should be labeled as pregame-safe, in-game-only, or postgame-descriptive. citeturn22view3turn22view4turn10view0

- **Starter, lineup, and injury provenance is a real missing system.** The resource set repeatedly shows that official lineup data are sparse or unofficial, and that working products still scrape, infer, or manually override starter information. FORGE would benefit from a small source-confidence layer attached to starters, lines, and injuries rather than treating them as equally trustworthy facts. citeturn19view0turn19view1turn20view0turn20view1

- **Your trend engine probably needs more windows and better early-season priors.** Good open models use multiple lookbacks such as 5, 10, 20, 40, and full-season windows, and some even split the season into early/middle/late regimes. If FORGE currently leans on one recent window or one season-to-date blend, it is probably missing stabilization opportunities, especially in October and November. citeturn10view0turn12view1turn11view2

- **Skater projection should probably separate stable skill from recent opportunity more aggressively.** The resources suggest that goals and assists alone are not enough; /60 rates, EV-only variants, expected-goals context, role/time-on-ice, and hierarchical partial pooling all matter. If you do not already have clear decomposition into talent, usage, and matchup context, that is a major “what you don’t know yet” area. citeturn5view1turn9view2turn2view0

- **Goalie projection needs explicit mixture uncertainty, not just a volatility note.** The evidence in your source set is too strong to justify a single thin starter-point estimate. You likely need starter probability distributions, wider predictive intervals, and perhaps scenario-weighted slate outputs when starter information is still soft. citeturn11view2turn27view0turn27view3turn17view3

- **Consistency should be operationalized carefully, not romanticized.** Team-level consistency is probably lower leverage than average performance, while player-level consistency is more useful when attached to metric stickiness and sample-size-aware uncertainty. If FORGE does not yet distinguish those cases, it risks displaying “trend” or “consistency” in a way that overstates signal. citeturn18view1turn2view0turn25view1

- **The model may still be underusing game-state and manpower context.** Your pipeline already ingests play-by-play and shifts, which is great. The gap is to ensure those data actually become predictive state variables—EV/PP/PK splits, shot-quality context, empty-net handling, and perhaps eventually live-state models or richer win/shutout simulation. citeturn3view1turn8view0turn23view3

- **Calibration coverage should be broader than “overall accuracy.”** Good public models expose log loss, Brier score, calibration, and often confidence-conditioned performance. If FORGE does not yet score itself by confidence bucket, starter-certainty bucket, stat type, and season segment, you know less than you think about where the model is actually strong. citeturn6view1turn10view0turn12view2turn13view1

- **The UI could explain changes better than it probably does today.** The product-side resources suggest users want not just projections, but reasons: injury timelines, starter certainty, source freshness, and visual trend context. If FORGE cannot explain *why* a projection changed since yesterday, that is a product gap even if the modeling is solid underneath. citeturn20view4turn21view0turn19view2

The Hockey-Graphs tag page is an archive rather than a single study; the injury-status Reddit thread is mostly a question without a useful technical resolution; and the Wildhockey thread is mainly a visualization prompt rather than an analytics method source.
